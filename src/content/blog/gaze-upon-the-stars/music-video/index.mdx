[Last time](/blog/gaze-upon-the-stars) I talked about how I created one of my best songs, _gaze upon

## Chapter 2: The Music Video

The animation was created in **Unreal Engine** <small>_(version **5.7.4**, to be exact)_</small>,
which happens to be the same engine Home Paige uses.

I had previously tried both Unity and Godot, but neither of them really ended up working out. Even
Unreal has its flaws; it has been rather buggy and even unstable at times, and their Linux support
isn't the best, especially since version 5.7. However, its tools for creating linear content do
compensate for that enough.

I unfortunately cannot share the whole Unreal Engine project due to it using some Fab assets, which
are not allowed to be redistributed. Also, since this was my first time using UE, there is a fair
bit of jank involved, which... isn't unusual for me, to be honest. With all of that aside, let's go
over how it was made, shall we?

	Just so you know, whenever I say **UE** or **Unreal**, I'm referring to **Unreal Engine**.
</small>

### Gazing upon the Stars

Since the song is titled "gaze upon the stars", obviously the sky needed some stars to gaze at. And
for some reason, UE5's default sky just doesn't have any of them, which is unfortunate. You might
say you can drop in the legacy sky blueprint left over from older versions of Unreal, which _does_
have stars, but those are **not animated**. And that's just lame. They do offer another solution,
the _Celestial Vault_ plugin, but it completely broke my lighting setup, so I couldn't use it.

I initially used a Fab asset called _Good Sky_ for this, which did have animated stars. However,
when I posted an
[early version of the music video](https://discord.com/channels/1414500915200917597/1482484409595855041/1482484409595855041)
in the Home Paige Discord server, people craved for more. They wanted shooting stars and
constellations. And Good Sky wasn't going to cut it for that. So I ended up doing it myself, by
making **my own sky material**.
